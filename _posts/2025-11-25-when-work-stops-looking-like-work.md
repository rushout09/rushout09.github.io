---
title: "When Work Stops Looking Like Work"
---

We are entering a strange new period where the definition of work is being rewritten in real time. Activities that once felt like distractions are becoming central to how people create value, and activities that once symbolized work are starting to look outdated.

For years, writing long stretches of code or reports was considered real work. It was concrete, visible, and measurable. But AI is changing that. The hard part is no longer typing. The hard part is understanding what to build, framing problems clearly, and guiding the system toward the right outcome.

The person who can think sharply and articulate instructions will produce far more than the person who grinds through endless implementation. Much of what used to count as “serious work” will soon feel like heavy manual labor.

Scrolling or posting once looked like leisure. Something people did to pass time. Today these platforms shape distribution, demand, culture, and even hiring. Being active there is no longer a side activity. It is becoming part of the job itself. Reading the mood of an audience, contributing to conversations, publishing ideas, and staying visible are now real professional activities.

We are still early in this shift, but the people who adapt will feel like they’re working less while achieving far more. The ones who cling to old signals of productivity will feel like they’re working harder while falling behind.
